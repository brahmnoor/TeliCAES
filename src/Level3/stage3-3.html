Stage 3-3 Using “it,” “this” and “which” to refer to previously mentioned information

  • A.“It” and “this” are frequently used to refer to previously mentioned ideas and information. These two pronouns are generally not interchangeable when used to refer to previously mentioned things, and misusing them can cause confusion for a reader. A key factor in deciding when to use “it” or “this” is the length or scope of information to which the writer wants to refer. For example:

    Steve Jobs started Apple at the age of 20. This completely surprised his parents. (‘This’ refers to the whole idea in the first sentence.)

    This view of the state's educative, paternalistic function is still widely adhered to by Hong Kong people, even though it might seem to sit uneasily alongside the democratic aspirations that many of them also harbor (‘it’ refer the view of the state’s educative, paternalistic function). (Vickers & Kan, 2005)

  • B. "Which" is a relative pronoun used to construct relative clauses. Relative clauses are used to give additional information about a thing mentioned in a sentence. The grammar of relative clauses is a separate topic and will not be covered here, but one feature of the usage of “which” is relevant to our discussion in this unit. The examples below illustrate that “which” can be used to refer to a previously mentioned noun or noun phrase, or can be used to refer to a previous clause or sentence. For example:

    The Students' Movement of the time, which played a part in pushing for the Chinese language to be granted official status, was inspired partly by anti-colonialist. (Vickers & Kan, 2005)

    During the colonial rule, the Chinese history curriculum encouraged political apathy on the part of students, which meant that it would not pose a threat to the colonial government. (Kan, 2010)
