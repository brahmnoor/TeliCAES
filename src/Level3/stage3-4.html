Stage 3-4 Using “it” as an “empty” reference, or a forward-looking reference to something to be discussed later in the sentence

  • In addition to being used to refer to previously mentioned ideas and information, “it” is frequently used in two other ways:

  • A. An “empty” reference or “dummy” subject.

  • When used as an empty or dummy reference, “it” does not refer to a specific noun or noun phrase in the text, instead ‘it’ refers to the general situation in which, or about which, the statement is made. For example:

  • 1. It appears that Asian travellers and Western travellers had significantly different perceptions of various attributes of air quality in general, and particularly the air quality in Hong Kong. (Law & Cheung, 2007)
  • 2. When it came to interpreting findings, there are a lot of important factors that need to be carefully considered.

  • For the two examples above, “it” refers to nothing more than the situation rather than to any specific word or phrase in the sentence.

  • In addition to being used to refer to previously mentioned ideas and information, “it” is frequently used in two other ways:

  • B. An “anticipatory” reference

  • “It” is also used as an “anticipatory” reference to information that comes later in the sentence. For example:

  • 1. It has long been a prevalent belief among the Chinese that effective government and education go hand-in-hand.
  • 2. It is striking that all reference to the aim of encouraging, let alone "enforcing," a sense of local identity was omitted from official documents from the mid-1990s onward.

  • The function or purpose of using “it” as an anticipatory reference is to highlight or emphasize the idea being referred to. The sentences above could be re-written without an anticipatory “it,” but the prominence and clarity of the main idea that “it” serves to highlight would be weakened.

  • Use of “it” as an anticipatory subject to highlight an idea later in the sentence is a common way to emphasize a statement in academic writing. It is also used in academic writing with the passive voice to create “objective-sounding” statements:

  • It is generally thought that the Hong Kong government's air pollution index does not accurately reflect the level of risk to respiratory health caused by air pollution in the city.
