Stage 7-1 Nominalization in Academic Writing

What is normalization?

Nominalization is a word formation process in which nouns are created or derived from words of other parts of speech, for instance verbs and adjectives. Read the two tables below:

Read the following pairs of sentences before and after the process of nominalization:
  • 81% of delegates voted to urge a broader membership to approve the contract.
  • 81% of delegates voted for urging a broader membership for the approval of the contract.
  • The university has made a difficult decision to eliminate certain positions.
  • The elimination of certain positions is a difficult decision made by the university.
  • Students are expected to revise their academic essays a few times before submission.
  • Students are expected to make several rounds of revisions to their academic essays before submission.
  • Student should always be aware that the goal of fluency practice is to better understand the meaning of what one is reading (Kuhn & Stahl, 2003, as cited in Olson, 2011, p.158).
  • Students should always develop an awareness that the goal of fluency practice is to better understand the meaning of what one is reading (Kuhn & Stahl, 2003, as cited in Olson, 2011, p.158).
  • For students who take medications at school, the teacher and school nurse are responsible for working with parents to ensure that these young people have an adequate supply of the drugs (Johns & McGrath, 2014, pp.39-40).
  • For students who take medications at school, it is the teacher’s and school nurse’s responsibility for working with parents to ensure that these young people have an adequate supply of the drugs (Johns & McGrath, 2014, pp.39-40).
  • It is believed that formal training regarding how to effectively implement technology into the classroom is scarce (Kim, Jain, Westhoff, & Rezabek, 2008, p.277)..
  • It is believed that there is a scarcity of formal training regarding how to effectively implement technology into the classroom(Kim, Jain, Westhoff, & Rezabek, 2008, p.277). .

1. It is rather simple to generate nouns in the English language. For example, -ing can be added to a verb which can then become a noun (aka gerund): “seeing is believing”, “blowing bubbles is what children usually like”, “Susie tactfully avoided driving off the beach”.

2. Likewise, attaching suffixes such as -ism, -ness and -ity to an adjective can transform the adjective itself into a noun: “The rapid growth of imperialism raises public awareness and increases the probability of a nuclear war”.

3. Sometimes, nominalization can be done by placing a definite article i.e. “the” in front of an adjective forming noun phrases like the deaf, the injured, the poor, the unemployed, etc.

4. On the other hand, nominalization can occur by simply converting verbs or adjectives into nouns without the need for changing the word form or adding extra lexical elements. Read the two tables below (Huddleston & Pullum, 2002, pp.1641-1642):

Read the following pairs of sentences for both “verb to noun conversions” and “adjective to noun conversions”:

  • Demands for standardized learning control lesson plans and classroom activities, which leads to an equal demand for measurable methods of imparting knowledge and improving student performance (Stitt & Pula, 2014, p.26)
  • Demands for standardized learning have control over lesson plans and classroom activities, which leads to an equal demand for measurable methods of imparting knowledge and improving student performance(Stitt & Pula, 2014, p.26).
  • Pupils usually desire to learn what will be of assistance in their life's endeavours
  • Pupils usually have a desire to learn what will be of assistance in their life's endeavours.
  • It may be advantageous for lower performing students to assist partners who have not previously read a particular text (Olson, 2011, p.160).
  • It may be advantageous for lower performing students to assist partners who have not previously had a read of a particular text (Olson, 2011, p.160).
  • Opportunity to practise early phonological skills in the preschool classroom may have a positive impact on pupils’ later reading ability (Gischlar & Vesay, 2014).
  • The positive of practising early phonological skills in the preschool classroom is the probable facilitation of pupil’s later reading ability (Gischlar & Vesay, 2014).
