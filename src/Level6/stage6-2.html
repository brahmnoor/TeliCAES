Stage 6-2 Functions of Hedges

According to Hyland (1998), academic writers use hedges for the following purposes:

  • 1. Protect your claim from possible attack
  • 2. Demonstrate doubt
  • 3. Express tentativeness
  • 4. Discuss a possibility
  • 5. Show that information is not a verified fact
  • 6. Enable the writer to make claims that are proportionate to the evidence available at the time of writing

Compare the examples below:

  • Student A: The rapid growth of higher education decreases the quality of students entering tertiary education.
  • Student B: The rapid growth of higher education appears to decrease the quality of students entering tertiary education.

Now compare another pair of examples:

  • The low recognition of the associate's degree was probably due to the perceived insufficient quality of these programs.
  • The low recognition of the associate's degree could have been due to the perceived insufficient quality of these programs.

The two sentences above are identical, except for the hedging languages. Is there any difference between “probably” and “could have been”? Which one shows stronger evidence?

Click on this link to show the explanation.

In fact, “probably” implies that the writer has some evidence to his / her claim, which has been argued earlier in this paragraph. However, “could have been” indicates that the writer is making an assumption based on what has been argued earlier in the paragraph. Comparing the meanings of these hedges in the two sentences, one can say that Sentence 1 has stronger evidence for this claim. In other words, writers need to match the evidence they have for that claim when selecting hedging languages.
